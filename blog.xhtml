We have gathered our texts! And this past week we spent some time individually marking them up for what we’re calling “divergences.” Based on our preliminary mark ups and based on some linguistic papers that give guidelines on how to mark up errors in writing and in speech, we’re going to sit down tomorrow and finalize how we’re going to categorize and code these divergences.

Some of our thoughts so far: So we’re using the term “divergences” because the mark up we do isn’t going to explicitly be error mark up. First of all, what is an error, i.e., what needs to be corrected to make the sentence grammatical, is an arbitrary decision. For example, “The boy have a bicycle.” Depending on how you look at it, the error could be that the subject NP is not plural OR the verb is not correctly conjugated (“The boys have a bicycle” versus “The boy has a bicycle”).

To compensate for this and to compensate for the unwieldy amount of divergences we’re sure to find in at least the Slovak translations (Google translate handles German fairly well!), we have decided to sit down tomorrow and try out the idea of marking all the verbs in both translations -- and in the process, we’ll necessarily identify all the clauses and sentences as well -- and then we’ll mark whether or not the gTranslated verb differs from the official English version. Finally, we plan to note how the verbs differ.... are they a synonym for what’s in the official English, but still grammatically meaningful? Are they lexically incorrect? Do they differ in tense, subject, direct object?

Finally, when once we accumulate our data, for each translation, we can give ratios and report on the number of divergences that appeared per sentence, per paragraph, per passage, per chapter, etc. And we can give percentages on what percentage of divergences in each translation was still grammatical versus ungrammatical, and on what kind of grammaticality existed.

Fri, 02/21/2014

etf7

For our project, we have a very interesting aspect that is diversity within the same end goal. It isn't the easiest thing to work with four different languages, and then try to compare the results. To do so, this is why we are using the English text of Harry Potter, translated into our foreign languages of choice, then back into English. Also, it is super relevant because of the primary use of Google Translate. Most of the time, users will be taking a foreign language and translating it into their own, which is what we are doing in our project! This past week we have been able to mark up the translations of our texts. In our last meeting we wrote our Relax NG schema for the XML documents that we are making for each of the languages.

We have the tag 's' around each sentence, with the mandatory attributes "in" for insertion, "del" for deletion, "tr" for transposition, and "diff" for difference. It is then within each sentence that we can specifically use the tag 'diff' to specify what the differences are. For each tag of 'diff', we have to choose one of the following attributes, "det", "n", "v", "adj", "adv", "pp", "pro", respectively based on the part of speech. We then also have to choose a type attribute for the 'diff', which will be one of the following, "ten", "cas", "num", "gen", "mst", or "moo". These are the tense, case, number, gender, mistranslate, and mood errors. This is a great way to tag our documents so that we will all be able to compare our findings when we are all done with the same sections.
